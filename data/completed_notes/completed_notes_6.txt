<h1>Hyperbolic Functions: Differentiation, Inverse Operations, and Identities</h1>
<p style="color:whitesmoke;">
<strong>Hyperbolic functions</strong> extend the classical trigonometric functions into the complex domain and are defined in terms of exponential functions. The common hyperbolic functions include <strong>sinh</strong> (hyperbolic sine), <strong>cosh</strong> (hyperbolic cosine), <strong>tanh</strong> (hyperbolic tangent), <strong>coth</strong> (hyperbolic cotangent), <strong>sech</strong> (hyperbolic secant), and <strong>csch</strong> (hyperbolic cosecant). Their derivatives and relationships play a key role in calculus, particularly for solving integrals, differential equations, and modeling various physical and engineering systems. This section details the differentiation of these functions, the behavior of their inverses, and their fundamental identities, demonstrated through stepwise calculation sequences and formulaic verifications.
</p>

<h3>Differentiation of Hyperbolic and Inverse Hyperbolic Functions</h3>
<p style="color:whitesmoke;">
Calculating the derivative of a function involving hyperbolic expressions, especially when the argument itself is a function (<code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">\varphi(x)</code>), requires the <strong>chain rule</strong>. For inverse hyperbolic functions, the derivative formula arises from implicit differentiation and often involves algebraic simplification.
</p>
<ol>
  <li>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;"><strong>Differentiating the Inverse Hyperbolic Sine:</strong> For <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">y = \sinh^{-1}(\varphi(x))</code>, the derivative is: </p>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
    <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
      \frac{d}{dx} \left( \sinh^{-1}(\varphi(x)) \right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (\varphi(x))^2}}\cdot\varphi'(x)
    </code>
    </p>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;">This formula generalizes to any composite argument. For example, if <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">\varphi(x) = 4x</code>:</p>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
      <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
        y' = \frac{d}{dx}(4x)\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+(4x)^2}}
      </code>
    </p>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
      The derivative is constructed by multiplying the derivative of the inner function <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">4</code> by the radical denominator adjusted for the squared argument.
    </p>
  </li>
  <li>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;"><strong>Differentiating Hyperbolic and Related Composite Functions:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          Example: <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">y = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 9x}}</code>. The process of derivation yields:
        </p>
        <ul>
          <li>
            <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
              Using the chain rule, differentiate with respect to <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">x</code>: 
            </p>
            <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
              <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
                y' = \frac{-1}{\sqrt{1+9x}} \cdot y^2 \cdot 9
              </code>
            </p>
          </li>
          <li>
            <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
              After simplification, the expression can be rewritten as:
              <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
                y' = \frac{9}{2\sqrt{1+9x}}
              </code>
            </p>
          </li>
        </ul>
      </li>
      <li>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          Another algebraic example: 
          <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
            y' = \frac{2\sqrt{x}}{2\sqrt{x}\cdot(4-x)}
          </code>
          which reduces to
          <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
            y' = \frac{2\sqrt{x}}{(4-x)}
          </code>
          under proper simplification.
        </p>
      </li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;"><strong>Product Rule with Inverse Hyperbolic Tangent:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          For <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">y = (20+50)\tanh^{-1}(\Theta+4)</code>:
        </p>
        <ol>
          <li>
            <p style="color:whitesmoke;">Let <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">f(x) = 20+50</code>, <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">g(x) = \tanh^{-1}(\Theta+4)</code>.</p>
          </li>
          <li>
            <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
              Apply the <strong>product rule</strong>:
              <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
                y' = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)
              </code>
            </p>
          </li>
          <li>
            <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
              The derivative of the inverse hyperbolic tangent function is:
              <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
                \frac{d}{dx}\left[\tanh^{-1}(h(x))\right] = \frac{1}{1 - (h(x))^2} h'(x)
              </code>
            </p>
          </li>
          <li>
            <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
              Plugging into the formula, the expanded form for the derivative is:
              <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
                y' = (20+55) \tanh^{-1}(\Theta+4) + \frac{\Theta+50}{1-(\Theta+4)^2}
              </code>
              <br>
              The stepwise computation often includes explicit differentiation of <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">\Theta</code> components and simplification of denominators.
            </p>
          </li>
        </ol>
      </li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;"><strong>Chain Rule with Inverse Hyperbolic Tangent and Trigonometric Argument:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          For <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">y = 9\tanh^{-1}(\cos x)</code>,
          using the chain rule and the inverse hyperbolic tangent derivative:
        </p>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
            y' = 9\left( \frac{1}{1-(\cos x)^2} \cdot (-\sin x) \right)
          </code>
        </p>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          Since <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">1-(\cos x)^2 = \sin^2 x</code>, this simplifies:
        </p>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
            y' = -9 \frac{\sin x}{\sin^2 x} = \frac{-9}{\sin x}
          </code>
        </p>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          Here, recognizing standard trigonometric identities is crucial for further reduction.
        </p>
      </li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ol>

<h3>Verification of Hyperbolic Identities and Derivative Rules</h3>
<p style="color:whitesmoke;">
<strong>Hyperbolic identities</strong> mirror those of trigonometric functions but have distinct algebraic characteristics, especially when expressed in terms of exponentials. Their verification follows algebraic manipulation and substitution of definitions.
</p>
<ol>
  <li>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;"><strong>Verifying the Definition of Hyperbolic Cotangent:</strong></p>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
      By definition,
      <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
        \coth(x) = \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{e^x - e^{-x}}
      </code>
      <br>
      Using hyperbolic cosine and sine definitions:
      <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
        \coth(x) = \frac{\cosh(x)}{\sinh(x)} = \frac{\frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2}}{\frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2}} = \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{e^x - e^{-x}}
      </code>
      <br>
      Algebraic manipulation and use of the definitions confirm the identity.
    </p>
  </li>
  <li>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;"><strong>Exploring Exponential Examples:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          For certain cases, algebraic confusion may be clarified by substituting exponentials and seeking cancellation or simplification. For instance:
        </p>
        <ul>
          <li><code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">e^x - e^{-x} = \frac{x}{e^x}</code> [note: symbolic simplifications]</li>
          <li><code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">\frac{2x}{e^x}</code> (highlighted as a boxed simplification)</li>
        </ul>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          Maintaining careful algebraic steps preserves clarity about which features cancel.
        </p>
      </li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;"><strong>Algebraic Relations among Hyperbolic Functions:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          Identities such as <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">\cosh^2(x) - \sinh^2(x) = 1</code> underlie the verification of many algebraic relationships:
          <ul>
            <li>
              <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
                \frac{\cosh^2(x)}{\cosh^2(x)} = 1
              </code>
            </li>
            <li>
              <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
                1 - \tanh^2(x) = \sech^2(x)
              </code>
              <br>
              which corresponds to the Pythagorean-like identity in hyperbolic functions.
            </li>
          </ul>
        </p>
      </li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li>
    <p style="color:whitesmoke;"><strong>Derivative of Hyperbolic Cotangent:</strong></p>
    <ol>
      <li>
        <p style="color:whitesmoke;">
          The <strong>quotient rule</strong> is applied for <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">\coth(x) = \frac{\cosh(x)}{\sinh(x)}</code>:
        </p>
        <ul>
          <li>
            <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
              \frac{d}{dx}\left(\coth(x)\right) = \frac{\sinh(x)\sinh(x) - \cosh(x)\cosh(x)}{\sinh^2(x)}
            </code>
          </li>
          <li>
            <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">
              = \frac{-1}{\sinh^2(x)} = -\csch^2(x)
            </code>
            <br>
            The simplification uses the core identity <code style="color:#00aaff; font-family: Menlo, Monaco, 'Courier New', monospace;">\cosh^2(x) - \sinh^2(x) = 1</code>.
          </li>
        </ul>
      </li>
    </ol>
  </li>
</ol>
